Fearing AI, I was reluctant to use ChatGPT. But friends, it changed my life

Newly diagnosed with ADHD, I now rely on ChatGPT to prioritise tasks without judgment – proof that AI can be both dangerous and life-changing.

By Van Badham — Wed 11 Sep 2024, 00.24 BST

It’s easy to be convinced that the myriad applications of AI pave a fun but nonetheless alarming digital path towards doom, doom, doom.

Let’s start with “slop”. It’s the term now used for AI-created graphic content pushed out on social media to attract engagement using spectacular, surreal images – the kind that can digitally mash Jesus with prawns in photographic detail.

Those not entranced by the bizarre can be dangerously tempted by beauty, in the proliferation of anonymous social media accounts posting AI-enhanced images celebrating western architecture and art. Historians and extremism researchers warn of a subtle pipeline promoting cultural superiority through aesthetic bait.

From skepticism to reliance

My personal anxieties about AI extended far beyond its misuse in politics or disinformation. As a writer, I discovered that my work had been used to train AI models without consent or compensation – a discouraging blow to any creator.

Yet, my relationship with AI changed after I was formally diagnosed with ADHD. In a moment of curiosity, I started using ChatGPT not as a threat but as an aid. Months later, I find myself relying on it daily: the technology helps me quiet the mental noise and manage tasks that once felt overwhelming.

How AI became my silent assistant

The “time blindness” that comes with ADHD often leads to overcrowded schedules and constant lateness. Now I outsource task prioritization to “the robot” on my phone. It calculates travel times, suggests routes, and even adjusts my itinerary based on real-time changes.

It remembers my dietary preferences, breaks down my exercise plan, and even advises me what to wear for the weather. Most importantly, it never judges me. For someone with ADHD, that’s transformative.

“One of ADHD’s principal tortures is shame in asking for help with simple tasks. The robot knows no judgment.”

The Australian government is now calling for guardrails on AI. Regulation is necessary, but so is recognizing that AI can also be an empowering tool when used responsibly.

I may still be wary of AI’s darker implications, but I can’t deny its impact on my life. As technology theorist Melvin Kranzberg once said: “Technology is neither good nor bad; nor is it neutral.” For me, it’s become a lifeline.
